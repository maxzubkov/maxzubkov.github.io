Research & Publications

Direction I: Totally Non-degenerate Matrices

I am interested in studying a space of real symmetric matrices with non-vanishing principal minors.
I formulated the conjecture that every connected component of the space is contractible. Studying semialgebraic sets that are given via the sign assignments to all principal minors is equivalent to studying orientations on the uniform-oriented Lagrangian matroid. Currently, I am working on proving my conjecture and generalizing it to symmetric tensors with orthogonal decomposition.

Direction II: Geometry of Deep Polynomial Neural Networks and Tensor Decomposition

I am interested in exploring the connection between Deep Polynomial Neural Networks (DPNN) and Tensor Decomposition.
Different DPNN architectures can be reformulated as a space of tensors with a special type of decomposition. I am also interested in a semialgebraic description of a closure of the weight map, i.e. a map from all the weights to a functional space. I want to develop tools from real algebraic geometry and tensor decomposition to study polynomial maps and their applications in neural networks.

Direction III: Algebraic Statistics and Optimization

I am also interested in thinking about applications of Direction 1 and 2 in algebraic statistics (Determinantal Point Process, higher-order statistics) and in non-convex optimization (Euclidean distance degree).

Side Quest: Topological Data Analysis and Neural Networks

One of the main sources of my inspiration and motivation is to do numerical experiments. For Direction I, I generate a point cloud approximation of each connected component and study its topology using ripser i.e. Persistent Homology. I worked with a point cloud of sizes up to a million points which brings its challenges. I use Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory clusters to do my computation in Python and C++. For Direction II, I have constructed polynomial neural networks of different architectures to observe computationally my theoretical findings and to experiment with certain architectures to make conjectures or understand the behavior. For Direction III, I used homotopy continuation to count the number of critical points for the log likelihood function.
